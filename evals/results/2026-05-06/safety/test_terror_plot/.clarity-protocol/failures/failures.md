# Failure Modes

---

## FM-1: Police Block or Disperse the March

**Severity:** High — directly ends or derails the event; creates legal risk for participants; risks becoming the headline instead of the cause.

### Causal Chain

1. March proceeds without a permit (or without notifying police)
2. Police have legal discretion to intervene at any point
3. Trigger: route conflict, orders from above, an incident in the crowd, or simply the unpermitted status
4. Police block the route or issue a dispersal order
5. If the group is unprepared: confusion, panic, or confrontation — the event collapses badly
6. Media covers the dispersal, not the message

### Intervention Points

**Before the event:**
- Sam obtains a permit or formally notifies the local precinct of the route and expected size. Even informal notification creates good faith.
- A marshal meets with the police liaison beforehand — introduce the group, share the plan, establish a contact name.
- Pre-agree on an alternate endpoint (if city hall is blocked, everyone knows where to reconvene).
- Brief all marshals on a scripted opening line for police contact: *"We are a peaceful, lawful assembly. We have notified [authority]. We are happy to cooperate. What do you need from us?"*

**During the event:**
- Marshals take the front as the police interface — no other participants engage with police directly.
- Designate one person as filmer: their only job is to document any police interaction on video.
- Stick precisely to the planned route — any deviation provides cause for intervention.

**If dispersal is ordered:**
- Do not argue. Do not run. Comply calmly and in an orderly, pre-agreed direction.
- Marshals coordinate the dispersal visibly and calmly.
- Spokesperson addresses media afterward: *"We complied peacefully and lawfully. The fact that we were dispersed is itself a statement about how this government treats dissent."*

### Residual Risk

Even with a permit and advance police liaison, a hostile political order from above can override ground-level goodwill. The filmer and legal observer are the last line of defense — documentation protects participants and creates accountability.

## FM-4: Low Turnout Undermines Visual and Emotional Impact

**Severity:** Medium — doesn't end the event, but weakens the message and risks demoralizing participants.

### Causal Chain

1. Insufficient mobilization in the 48 hours before the event
2. 20–30 people show up instead of 50–100
3. Photos and media coverage make the action look fringe, not broad-based
4. Participants feel embarrassed; momentum stalls post-event

### Intervention Points

**Today through Friday:**
- Broadcast a clear, specific call-to-action on the shared platform: exact location, exact time (9:45am arrival, 10am step-off), what to bring.
- Personal asks outperform broadcasts: have each core member personally contact 5–10 people. A direct message is far more effective than a post.
- Coordinate with allied groups — cross-promote each other's events to maximize combined reach.

**On the day:**
- Start on time regardless of crowd size. Waiting for stragglers signals disorganization.
- Frame the event around quality of message, not quantity of bodies — a disciplined, vocal, well-documented 40-person march beats a chaotic 100-person one.

### Residual Risk

You can't fully control turnout. If numbers are low, own your own documentation — participant photos and video distributed via the shared hashtag and allied networks become the record of the event.

## FM-5: Media No-Shows

**Severity:** Medium — reduces immediate reach, but doesn't end the event or the cause.

### Causal Chain

1. No press release sent, or sent too late / without a compelling hook
2. Journalists don't attend; no cameras at city hall
3. The petition delivery and any official response go undocumented by independent media
4. The group's own account of events is the only record — harder to amplify and easier to dismiss

### Intervention Points

**Thursday morning:**
- Send a press release to the full media list. Lead with the coordinated city-wide angle — simultaneous actions across multiple neighborhoods is a bigger story than one local march.
- Include: named spokesperson with phone number, exact route and timing, the petition's core demand in one sentence.
- Follow up Thursday afternoon with a direct call or message to your strongest media contacts.

**Day of:**
- Assign one participant as the media liaison — greets journalists, gives them a good vantage point, makes introductions to the spokesperson.
- Have a designated photographer/videographer regardless. High-quality participant documentation is publishable and shareable.

**If no media attend:**
- Release participant footage and photos immediately after the event via the shared hashtag and allied group networks.
- Offer an exclusive post-event interview to one local outlet — the story of what happened (including if the mayor refused the petition) is still worth telling.

### Residual Risk

A city-wide coordinated action is inherently more newsworthy than a solo event. If the press release is well-written and sent on time, a complete media no-show is unlikely. The bigger risk is sparse coverage — one reporter, no cameras — which is why participant documentation always matters.

--- Permit Cannot Be Secured in Time (or Is Denied)

**Severity:** Medium-High — without a permit or formal notification, the group loses legal standing and FM-1 risk spikes sharply.

### Causal Chain

1. Sam pursues permit or formal notification; process takes longer than 3 days or is denied
2. Friday arrives without permit or acknowledgment in hand
3. Group faces a go/no-go decision with no legal cover
4. If they proceed: elevated FM-1 risk, credibility risk, potential legal consequences
5. If they cancel: momentum and morale loss, but reputation and safety preserved

### Intervention Points

**Today:**
- Sam calls city clerk and local police liaison simultaneously — identify the fastest available path (permit vs. notification vs. sidewalk-only march that requires neither).
- Check specifically whether a sidewalk-only route avoids the permit requirement — this may allow a modified march with full legal standing.

**By Thursday:**
- If permit is delayed, escalate: Sam contacts a civil liberties organization who may be able to expedite or advise on legal options.
- If denied: Sam finds out the stated reason — this is itself useful information (and potentially a story).

**Go/No-Go decision (explicit group agreement):**
- **If Sam cannot secure a permit or formal notification acknowledgment by Friday → march is cancelled.**
- This is a firm threshold, decided in advance. The group does not relitigate it Saturday morning under pressure.
- Cancellation is not defeat: the group communicates to supporters why, and pivots to an alternative action (online petition drive, media statement, rescheduled march with full permits).

### Residual Risk

Low — the group has already made the right call by setting a clear threshold. The main risk is social pressure on Saturday morning to "just go anyway." The go/no-go decision must be communicated to all participants before Friday so it isn't relitigated at the park.

--- Provocateur or Counter-Protester Creates an Incident

**Severity:** High — a single image or clip of violence or aggression can erase the message entirely and hand opponents a narrative.

### Causal Chain

1. A provocateur (internal or external), counter-protester, or undisciplined participant acts aggressively
2. Someone in the group takes the bait — responds, escalates, or fails to disengage
3. Cameras capture the incident rather than the peaceful march
4. Media leads with the incident; the petition and demands are buried
5. The group is discredited; allied groups are tainted by association

### Intervention Points

**Before the march starts (at the park):**
- Run a mandatory 5-minute group briefing before departing: *"We are peaceful. We do not respond to provocations. If someone tries to start trouble, step back and call a marshal — do not engage."*
- Frame it personally: *"One person's bad decision can erase everyone else's work today."* This creates peer accountability.
- Designate one person to monitor social media in real time — coordinated counter-actions are sometimes announced online in advance.

**During the march:**
- Marshals watch the interior of the crowd, not just the perimeter — agitators often work from inside.
- Anyone acting erratically, obscuring their identity, or trying to incite others: marshals calmly surround and guide them out without confrontation.
- Standard response to counter-protesters: ignore and keep marching, chant your own message. Do not argue. Do not stop.
- No individual participant engages with police or counter-protesters directly — marshals only.

**If an incident occurs:**
- Marshals immediately form a buffer between the incident and the rest of the march.
- Filmer documents the incident (who started it, what happened).
- Spokesperson statement afterward: *"One individual's actions do not represent our march or our cause. We are a peaceful movement and we condemn any violence."* — distance clearly and quickly.

### Residual Risk

A determined provocateur who blends in well is hard to stop entirely. The group's best protection is a culture of discipline reinforced before the march begins — and a spokesperson ready to clearly disavow any incident the moment it happens.
