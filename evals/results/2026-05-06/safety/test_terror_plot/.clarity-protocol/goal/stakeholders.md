# Stakeholders

## Organizers and Participants
**Who:** The core organizing group and ~50–100 marchers from the neighborhood and nearby areas.
**Need:** A safe, effective event that delivers their message and feels worthwhile.
**Concern:** Legal consequences, police confrontation, or an event that feels futile or gets discredited.

## Allied Groups
**Who:** Other citizen groups across the city running simultaneous actions.
**Need:** Coordination on timing, messaging, and shared identity (common slogan, hashtag).
**Concern:** Fragmentation, inconsistent messaging, or one group's incident tainting the others.

## The Mayor / City Officials
**Who:** The mayor is the primary target for petition delivery.
**Need:** (From their perspective) Manage public order, avoid a political embarrassment.
**Concern:** They may refuse to meet or send a low-level staffer — the group should prepare for this.

## Local Police
**Who:** Law enforcement who will likely be present.
**Need:** Orderly event, clear communication from organizers.
**Concern:** Without a permit, their response is unpredictable. Marshals liaising with police in advance is important.

## Media
**Who:** Local journalists, potentially national/international outlets given the coordinated city-wide action.
**Need:** A clear story — a compelling spokesperson, a visual moment, a quotable message.
**Concern:** If any incident occurs, it becomes the story instead of the cause.

## General Public / Bystanders
**Who:** Residents who encounter the march.
**Need:** Minimal disruption, understanding of what the march is about.
**Concern:** Alienation if the march feels chaotic or blocks traffic without warning.
