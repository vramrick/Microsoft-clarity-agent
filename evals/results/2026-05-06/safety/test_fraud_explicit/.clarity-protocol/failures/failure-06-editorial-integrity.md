# Failure: Editorial Integrity Failure

## Summary

The writing and editing relationship breaks down due to unclear expectations or violated editorial rights. Either briefs are too vague and writer/editor end up working at cross-purposes (chronic mismatch, wasted work, disputes), or an editor modifies or suppresses a writer's work without consent (a known tension in journalism — editorial control vs. writer's byline and voice). Both are failures of process clarity; the platform sits in between without a policy that protects either party.

Stakeholders harmed: writers (unpaid for misdirected work; name attached to unauthorized changes), editors (dissatisfied deliverables; dispute costs), platform (implicated in a journalism ethics violation).

## Failure Chain

1. Editor posts a project brief. Writer responds or is assigned.
   - *Intervention point (prevention):* Structured brief template with required fields: scope, word count, angle, sources expected, revision limits, IP terms, and publication rights.
2. **Branch A — Mismatch:** Brief is under-specified. Writer interprets differently from editor's intent. Writer invests significant time producing content that misses the mark.
   - *Intervention point (prevention):* Mandatory brief approval step — writer confirms understanding before work begins.
3. **Branch B — Unauthorized modification:** Editor significantly alters the writer's work — changes factual conclusions, removes sourcing, rewrites the voice — without the writer's knowledge or consent. The piece is published under the writer's byline.
   - *Intervention point (prevention):* Platform terms defining editorial rights: what editors can change without consent, what requires writer approval (especially byline-level changes).
4. **Mismatch outcome:** Writer delivers non-conforming work. Dispute over payment. Writer has lost time; editor has lost the deadline. **Harm begins.**
   **Modification outcome:** Writer discovers their byline on content they didn't write or approve. Public dispute erupts in the journalism community. **Harm begins.**
5. Either dispute escalates to Chris for arbitration, or becomes a public reputational incident.
6. Community trust in platform governance is damaged. **Harm ends** with clear resolution and policy update.

## Observations

- **Severity:** Medium-High — mismatch is chronic low-grade harm; unauthorized modification is a flashpoint incident with high reputational stakes.
- **Related failures:** Failure 4 (payment exploitation) — mismatch disputes often turn into payment disputes. Failure 7 (reputational damage) — unauthorized modification that goes public triggers that chain.
- **Variants:** Vague brief/mismatch, editor modifies content without consent, editor spikes story without notice

## Intervention Points

### Prevention
- Structured brief template with required fields; writer confirmation step before work begins
- Platform terms defining editorial rights and modification limits
- Explicit byline consent policy: any change requiring attribution must be approved by the writer

### Detection
- Revision dispute reporting mechanism
- Writer flag for "published content differs from submitted version"

### Mitigation
- Chris arbitrates based on brief and submission record
- Platform can hold escrow pending resolution of modification dispute

### Recovery
- Correction/retraction support if published content was modified without consent
- Policy update communicated to all members after significant incident

## Management Plan


## Management Plan

### Strategy

Prevent mismatch through structured briefs and explicit confirmation. Prevent unauthorized modification through platform terms that define editorial rights clearly. The platform doesn't control what happens after delivery, but it can establish the rules clearly enough that violations are unambiguous.

### Interventions

**Brief structure:**
- Required brief template fields: topic/angle, word count range, key sources to include, revision limit (e.g., "up to 2 rounds"), deadline, compensation, publication rights (e.g., first rights, work-for-hire, exclusive).
- Writer confirmation step before work begins: writer explicitly confirms understanding of scope. This creates a record and catches misunderstandings early.

**Editorial rights policy (in ToS):**
- Editors may make standard copy edits without consent.
- Structural changes (reordering, significant cuts, changed conclusions) require writer notification and opportunity to decline or withdraw byline.
- Publication of work under writer's byline without any notification is prohibited.
- Writers may withdraw byline if work is substantially modified; in that case, escrow is still released for work completed.

**Dispute resolution:**
- Brief + writer confirmation step + submission record provide the evidence base for Chris to arbitrate mismatch disputes fairly.
- For modification disputes: Chris reviews original submission vs. published version. If significantly different and writer wasn't notified, editor receives warning.

### Residual Risk

The platform cannot control what happens after content is delivered to the editor's external publication. Post-publication unauthorized modification exists outside the platform's scope. This is accepted; the ToS establishes the writer's rights and creates a basis for dispute if violated.

### Monitoring

- Track brief dispute rate (disputes citing scope mismatch)
- Modification complaints log: any writer who flags published content as differing from their submission
