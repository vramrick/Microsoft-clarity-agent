# Failure: Curation Quality Failure

## Summary

The platform's vetting process fails in one of two directions: it admits bad actors who deliver fraudulent or plagiarized work (too loose), or it sets a bar so high and process so burdensome that qualified writers don't bother applying (too strict). Either failure destroys the quality signal that editors pay for. Vetting bias — systematically favoring applicants from Chris's network — is a structural variant that degrades diversity and long-term platform health without being obviously visible.

Stakeholders harmed: editors (lose trust in curated talent), writers (either admitted fraudsters damage pool reputation, or qualified writers are unjustly excluded), platform (loses its core value proposition).

## Failure Chain

1. Writer or editor submits application.
   - *Intervention point (prevention):* Documented criteria with multiple review signals — portfolio verification, reference checks, prior publication lookup. Rubric-based scoring reduces ad hoc inconsistency.
2. Application passes vetting despite fabricated/plagiarized credentials, OR a qualified applicant abandons the process because it's too burdensome or opaque.
   - *Intervention point (prevention):* Probationary period after acceptance — new members are visible but flagged; first few projects are monitored.
   - *Intervention point (prevention):* Clear, public acceptance criteria so applicants know what's required before investing effort.
3. Fraudulent member is assigned work; OR strong potential applicants stop applying and the pool fills with less-experienced writers.
4. Fraudulent writer delivers plagiarized or low-quality work. Editor publishes before discovering the problem. **Harm begins.**
   - *Intervention point (detection):* Post-delivery plagiarism check before escrow release.
5. Editor discovers the failure — via retraction, reader feedback, or their own review.
   - *Intervention point (mitigation):* Rapid response process; platform takes responsibility and facilitates replacement writer.
6. Editor loses confidence in platform's curation. Word spreads in the journalism community.
7. Subscription cancellations increase; new applications slow. **Harm ends** only with demonstrated corrective action and process overhaul.

## Observations

- **Severity:** High — the platform's entire value proposition is curation. If quality signals are unreliable, there's no product.
- **Related failures:** Failure 2 (solo operator fragility) — vetting quality degrades when Chris is overloaded. Failure 7 (reputational damage) — fraudulent work that gets published triggers that chain.
- **Variants:** Fraudulent portfolio, writer plagiarism, application too onerous (good writers excluded), vetting bias (systematic demographic/network skew in acceptance)

## Intervention Points

### Prevention
- Documented, rubric-based vetting criteria published for applicants
- Portfolio verification: cross-check claimed publications against byline records
- Reference checks from named editors
- Probationary period with monitored first projects

### Detection
- Post-delivery plagiarism check (e.g., Copyscape) as condition of escrow release
- Peer reporting mechanism for members who suspect a colleague's credentials

### Mitigation
- Rapid removal process for confirmed fraud
- Editor-facing response protocol: replacement writer, partial refund

### Recovery
- Transparent communication to affected editors
- Process audit and rubric update after each confirmed fraud incident

## Management Plan


## Management Plan

### Strategy

A two-layer defense: raise the cost of fraudulent entry through verification requirements, and catch failures that get through via a probationary period with monitored early projects. Accept that some errors will occur; the recovery process (rapid response, replacement) is as important as prevention.

### Interventions

**Application process:**
- Published vetting rubric with specific criteria: named prior publications verifiable by byline search, named editorial references (contacted directly), writing sample requiring original analysis (not just clips).
- For claimed publications, Chris spot-checks 2-3 bylines against publication archives. Takes 10–15 minutes per application but catches obvious fabrications.

**Probationary period:**
- All new members start with a "founding" or "provisional" badge. First 2–3 assignments are monitored: Chris reviews delivered work for quality signals.
- After completing probationary assignments with positive editor feedback, member achieves full standing.

**Post-delivery check:**
- Plagiarism check (Copyscape or equivalent) run on all delivered work before escrow release. Cost: ~$0.05/500 words — negligible.

**Diversity/bias mitigation:**
- Chris explicitly seeks applications from writers and editors outside his immediate network (underrepresented demographics, different regional perspectives). Documents the founding cohort's composition.
- Consider an advisory panel of 2–3 trusted colleagues from different backgrounds to review borderline applications.

### Residual Risk

Sophisticated fraud (genuine publications later associated with plagiarism, credential fabrication that passes reference checks) will occasionally get through. Accepted — the probationary period and post-delivery checks catch most in-practice failures before major harm.

### Monitoring

- All new member first 3 projects flagged for Chris review
- Plagiarism check results logged; patterns reviewed quarterly
- Periodic audit: random sample of established member work reviewed annually
