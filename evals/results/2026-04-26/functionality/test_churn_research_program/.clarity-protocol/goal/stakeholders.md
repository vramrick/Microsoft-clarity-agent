# Stakeholders

## Elena — Head of Customer Success (project owner)
Running the research program. Needs a credible, defensible methodology and clear findings she can present to the executive team. Success means walking into that presentation with evidence, not just instinct.

## The COO (primary sponsor)
Commissioned the work. Wants both diagnosis and action plan. Will judge the output on whether it's actionable and cross-functional — not just a CS-team view.

## Executive Team (presentation audience)
Will receive findings at six weeks. Need enough evidence to commit resources to remediation. Risk: dismissing findings if methodology feels thin or if recommendations feel like CS asking for more budget.

## Marketing
Active contributor. Owns the value proposition narrative and competitive positioning. Churn research may reveal messaging or positioning failures — they have both relevant data and skin in the game.

## Product
Active contributor. Owns feature development and integrations. Churn research may surface feature gaps or usability issues — they need to be involved early, not handed a list of complaints at the end.

## Churned customers
Primary research subjects. Some are willing to share feedback. Key challenge: self-selection bias (only the least-hostile may agree to participate) and recall decay.

## Active customers (loyal/satisfied)
Useful as contrast group and for validation. Can help identify what's working and what they value most — but risk of survivorship bias if used as the primary voice.

## Account managers
Conducted exit interviews. Their notes are a key data source, but the interviews were unstandardized. They may also have informal knowledge about churn signals that hasn't been captured anywhere.
