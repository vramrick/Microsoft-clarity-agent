# Failure: Cross-Functional Resistance

## Summary

Marketing, product, or other departments block, dilute, or distort the research findings because the findings implicate their work. This can be active rejection ("that's not our problem"), passive deflection ("it's already on the roadmap"), or political competition for credit and blame. The result is that honest findings get softened before the executive presentation, or cross-functional ownership of recommendations never materializes, and remediation stalls.

## Failure Chain

1. The research program surfaces findings that implicate marketing (value proposition not landing), product (feature gaps, integration gaps), or both.
2. These findings are shared with marketing and product in cross-functional workshops — either as fully-formed conclusions or as emerging themes.
   - *Observation:* If workshops happen late (week 5), findings are already fixed and the sharing feels like notification rather than collaboration. Departments become defensive rather than engaged.
   - *Intervention point (prevention):* Share emerging themes early (week 3), before they're conclusions. Framing as "we're seeing patterns — does this match what you're seeing?" invites collaboration. Framing as "here's what we found" invites defensiveness.
3. Marketing reframes the finding as a CS execution problem. Product says features are "on the roadmap" without committing to timelines. Political dynamics lead each department to protect its own narrative.
   - *Intervention point (prevention):* Get COO sponsorship before workshops. If the COO communicates that this is a shared organizational diagnosis (not a CS audit of other departments), departments have less room to deflect.
   - *Intervention point (prevention):* In the workshop design, give each department a role in shaping the recommendations — not just reacting to findings. "Help us figure out what marketing can do about this" is more productive than "here's what we think marketing should do."
4. Workshop output is diplomatically softened. The synthesis reflects the negotiated version, not the interview evidence.
   - *Observation:* This is a critical branch point. Elena can either preserve the finding and accept the friction, or soften it to preserve relationships. The right choice depends on organizational culture, but the cost of softening should be explicit.
   - *Intervention point (mitigation):* Document the original finding and the departmental objection separately. Present both to the exec team, with a note that there is active disagreement. Let the exec team arbitrate rather than pre-arbitrating in the synthesis.
5. **Harm begins.** The executive presentation omits or minimizes the most important findings. Remediation plans address surface symptoms, not root causes.
6. Departments "own" recommendations that they don't actually believe in and don't implement. Churn continues. **Harm ends** when the second churn review cycle surfaces the same findings again.

## Observations

- **Severity:** High — Cross-functional resistance is the most organizationally complex failure mode and the hardest to prevent through research design alone. It requires leadership engagement, not just good methodology.
- **Related failures:** Closely linked to Failure 4 (Program Execution Breakdown) — late workshops are a key risk factor. Also linked to Failure 6 (Post-Program Failure) — politically-negotiated recommendations rarely get implemented with conviction.
- **Variants (from brainstorm):**
  - Marketing rejects findings that implicate messaging failures
  - Product dismisses feature gaps as "already on the roadmap"
  - Internal politics — departments compete for credit or deflect blame
  - Research findings fuel cross-functional blame rather than shared ownership

## Intervention Points

### Prevention
- Secure explicit COO sponsorship that frames this as a shared organizational diagnosis
- Involve marketing and product as contributors from week 3, not week 5
- Design workshops to give departments a role in shaping recommendations, not just reacting to findings

### Detection
- If a departmental objection is significant enough to change the synthesis, treat it as a signal to escalate rather than to revise
- Track workshop outputs: if every departmental "contribution" is a reframe of your findings, the collaboration isn't working

### Mitigation
- Document original findings and departmental objections separately; surface both to the exec team
- Frame cross-functional misalignment as a finding in its own right (it tells you something about the organization)

### Recovery
- If findings are softened post-presentation and churn continues: the second research cycle has more credibility — you can point to the previous program and note what wasn't acted on
