# Failure: Program Execution Breakdown

## Summary

The research program doesn't complete as planned — the data audit overruns, interview recruitment takes longer than expected or yields too few participants, workshops slip, or the COO redirects the program mid-flight. Any of these collapses the carefully staged six-week timeline. The final deliverable is rushed, incomplete, or misaligned with what was promised.

## Failure Chain

1. Program is scoped with tight week-by-week allocations, assuming each phase completes on schedule.
2. In week 1, the data audit reveals unexpected complexity: analytics export is blocked (triggering Failure 3), CRM cleanup takes longer than estimated, or interview recruitment outreach yields no early responses.
   - *Intervention point (prevention):* Build buffer into weeks 1–2. Don't schedule interviews before day 8. Treat the data audit as a variable-length gate, not a fixed phase.
   - *Intervention point (prevention):* Launch interview outreach in the first 48 hours of the program — recruitment is the longest lead-time activity and runs in parallel with the data audit.
3. Week 2 ends with the data audit still incomplete and fewer than half the required interview participants confirmed.
   - *Intervention point (detection):* Set a week 2 checkpoint: if fewer than 6 churned customer interviews are confirmed, escalate recruitment strategy (expand outreach, use different warm paths, consider incentives).
4. Interview phase is compressed. Workshops are scheduled late because participants aren't available, or are bumped due to competing priorities.
   - *Intervention point (prevention):* Schedule workshops in week 1 (calendar blocks, not just "plan to schedule"). Even a placeholder on marketing and product calendars prevents the worst slippage.
5. Partway through, the COO asks for additional scope or materially different outputs in response to an external event.
   - *Intervention point (prevention):* At program kickoff, agree on a change management process: any scope change must go through Elena with explicit acknowledgment of what gets dropped or delayed. Get COO sign-off on this process at the start.
   - *Intervention point (mitigation):* If scope changes mid-program, explicitly negotiate the trade-off: "We can add X, but that means Y gets cut or the deadline slips." Don't silently absorb new requests.
6. **Harm begins.** The synthesis phase is compressed to days rather than weeks. Findings are under-analyzed. The presentation is assembled under pressure.
7. The executive presentation presents preliminary findings as if they were complete, or the scope gap is visible and the presentation falls flat.
8. **Harm ends** — but the credibility cost to Elena and the CS team persists.

## Observations

- **Severity:** High — Timeline breakdown is the most likely single cause of a failed program outcome. It's a cascade risk: one phase slipping compresses every subsequent phase.
- **Related failures:** Strongly linked to Failure 3 (Data Access Failure), which is a common trigger. Also interacts with Failure 5 (Cross-Functional Resistance) — if workshops are scheduled late, cross-functional involvement becomes superficial.
- **Variants (from brainstorm):**
  - Data audit overruns weeks 1–2
  - Interview recruitment fails — not enough churned customers respond
  - Cross-functional workshops slip to week 5
  - COO shifts expectations mid-program

## Intervention Points

### Prevention
- Launch interview outreach in the first 48 hours — it has the longest lead time
- Schedule workshop calendar blocks in week 1
- Build 2–3 day buffers into each phase transition
- Agree on a scope change process with the COO at kickoff

### Detection
- Week 2 checkpoint: if fewer than 6 interviews confirmed, escalate immediately
- Week 3 checkpoint: if data audit is still ongoing, make an explicit cut and proceed with what's available

### Mitigation
- If timeline slips, cut scope explicitly (e.g., drop the contrast interviews, narrow to one priority segment) rather than compressing all phases equally
- Negotiate trade-offs transparently with the COO when scope changes arrive

### Recovery
- If the presentation deadline can't be met with full findings: present a "first phase" diagnosis with a clear plan for follow-on work — a partial answer honestly framed is better than a forced complete one
