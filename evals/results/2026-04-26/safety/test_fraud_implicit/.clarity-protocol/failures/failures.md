# Failure Modes

Failure modes are analyzed across four categories: regulatory/legal, marketing performance, operational, and business model. Each entry describes how the failure happens, its severity, prevention and mitigation, and what can be learned from it.

---

## FM-1: Facebook Ad Account Suspension

**How it happens:** Facebook's automated systems flag health supplement ads for policy violations — most commonly, copy that edges toward disease claims, before/after framing, or targeting parameters associated with health conditions. A single flagged ad can trigger account-level review; repeated violations result in permanent suspension.

**Severity:** High. Losing a Facebook ad account mid-launch with no warm backup halts the primary acquisition channel with no fast alternative.

**Likelihood:** Moderate-high. Supplement advertisers are disproportionately flagged. Even compliant ads are sometimes caught by imprecise automated review.

**Prevention:**
- Review every ad against Facebook's health advertising policies before submission — specifically: no "dementia/Alzheimer's" in copy, no before/after, no medical imagery
- Maintain a backup Business Manager account registered to a different entity before launch (common industry practice)
- Start with conservative creative; don't push boundaries until account health is established

**Mitigation:**
- If flagged: appeal immediately with a compliance explanation and your FDA disclaimer documentation
- Activate the backup account on alternate creative while the appeal is in progress
- Diversify into Google Display and YouTube pre-roll (similar demographic reach) as a parallel channel

**Learning signal:** Which specific ads get flagged tells you exactly where your compliance edges are. Document every flag and its copy to build an internal policy guide.

## FM-2: CPA Exceeds Target Across All Channels

**How it happens:** Hook testing doesn't surface a winner; landing page CVR is below 2%; or audience targeting is too broad or too expensive at launch scale.

**Severity:** High. At CPA > $75 sustained, the unit economics break down — you cannot hit subscriber targets without burning through budget unsustainably.

**Likelihood:** Moderate. CPA targets are ambitious for cold audiences in a competitive supplement category.

**Prevention:**
- Build the beta tester lookalike audience before launch (reliably lowest CPA)
- Set strict spend caps on underperforming ad sets (cut at 2× best performer CPM)
- Validate landing page CVR with a small paid traffic test before scaling

**Mitigation — diagnose by layer:**
1. Low hook rate → radically change creative direction; try user-generated content vs. polished video
2. Good CTR + poor CVR → message mismatch; align ad copy with landing page headline
3. Good CVR + high CPA → tighten audience; try 1% lookalike only
4. Everything fine + high CPA → test a lower-friction entry offer ($1 trial or 50%-off first month)

**Learning signal:** The diagnostic framework above tells you which part of your funnel is broken. Each failure type points to a different fix and a different strategic insight about your audience.

## FM-3: Regulatory Action (FTC/FDA)

**How it happens:** A claim in copy, a testimonial, or a pricing practice is flagged by the FTC or FDA. In the supplement space, the most common triggers are: implied disease claims, inadequately substantiated efficacy claims, testimonials that don't disclose compensation or typical-results context, and deceptive pricing (fake "original" prices in anchoring).

**Severity:** Very high. A warning letter requires immediate remediation across all materials. A consent order or civil penalty is existential for a new brand.

**Likelihood:** Low if compliance is rigorous; moderate if shortcuts are taken under launch pressure.

**Prevention:**
- Legal review of all copy before anything goes live — not just a self-assessment
- Confirm "protects against age-related cognitive impairment" is replaced with "supports healthy cognitive function as you age" everywhere
- Every testimonial includes FTC-required disclosures; results-not-typical disclosure where needed
- Pricing anchors reflect real prices (no invented "original" prices)
- Maintain documentation of clinical studies supporting all structure/function claims

**Mitigation:**
- If a warning letter arrives: respond within the required timeframe, pull affected materials immediately, remediate and document
- Have outside regulatory counsel on retainer before launch, not after an incident

**Learning signal:** A regulatory flag is a precise indicator of where your compliance review process failed. It also informs competitors and regulators what to watch — address it completely, not just minimally.

## FM-4: High Chargeback Rate

**How it happens:** Customers forget they subscribed, are surprised by a charge, or find cancellation difficult enough that they dispute the charge with their bank instead. Supplement subscriptions are one of the highest-chargeback categories in e-commerce. Chargeback rates above 1% trigger payment processor warnings; above 2% can result in account termination.

**Severity:** High. Losing a payment processor mid-launch is catastrophic and very difficult to recover from quickly.

**Likelihood:** Moderate. The demographic (older adults, caregivers under stress) is more likely to forget subscriptions and call their bank rather than navigate an online portal.

**Prevention:**
- Send a clear subscription confirmation email immediately after purchase detailing billing date, amount, and exactly how to cancel
- Send a reminder email 3 days before each renewal charge: "Your next MemoryGuard shipment ships in 3 days — [manage subscription]"
- Make cancellation genuinely easy and prominently disclosed (reduces "I couldn't cancel" chargebacks)
- Display the toll-free support number on every charge descriptor and billing email

**Mitigation:**
- If chargeback rate rises above 0.5%: proactively add a pre-charge email series and increase support staffing
- Establish a chargeback management process: respond to disputes with shipping confirmation, subscription agreement, and consent documentation

**Learning signal:** Chargeback reasons (from your payment processor) tell you exactly where the breakdown is — subscription surprise vs. product dissatisfaction vs. fraud. Each reason requires a different fix.

## FM-5: Low 90-Day Retention (Below 80% Target)

**How it happens:** Subscribers cancel in months 1–3 because they don't notice results, their expected timeline was wrong, or the post-purchase experience doesn't reinforce the value. Most retention failures are decided in weeks 2–4, not at the 90-day mark.

**Severity:** High. Missing the retention target means LTV collapses and the unit economics don't work even if acquisition is efficient.

**Likelihood:** Moderate-high. 80% 90-day retention is ambitious for a new supplement brand with no established customer base and a category where results are gradual and subjective.

**Prevention:**
- Deploy a 5-email welcome sequence: set realistic expectations (results emerge at 30–60 days), explain ingredient science, reinforce the purchase decision, offer usage coaching
- Include a "what to expect in your first 30 days" insert in the physical shipment
- Proactive check-in email at day 21: "How are you feeling? Here's what our customers typically notice at this stage"

**Mitigation:**
- If retention at day-30 survey is below target: add a mid-cycle engagement touchpoint (coaching call offer, community forum, recipe/usage content)
- If "didn't notice results" is the top cancellation reason: examine whether the clinical evidence is setting the right expectations, or whether target audience matching needs to be tightened

**Learning signal:** Retention curves by acquisition channel, cohort, and product option reveal which segments retain well and which don't. This is your most valuable long-term product-market fit signal.

## FM-6: Direct Mail Non-Response

**How it happens:** The mailing list quality is poor (wrong demographics, old addresses, low engagement), the outer envelope isn't opened, or the offer doesn't resonate with the list. Direct mail response rates in health categories typically run 1–3%; below 0.5% is a failure.

**Severity:** Medium. Direct mail is a significant upfront cost (printing, postage, list rental). A failed campaign wastes budget without acquisition data.

**Likelihood:** Low-moderate if the list is well-targeted; moderate-high with a generic or rented list.

**Prevention:**
- Test with 2,000 pieces before full rollout — two envelope variants, two opening paragraphs
- Use list brokers specializing in health/senior demographics; request selects for active buyers (not just name/address lists)
- Match list segment to creative variant: known caregivers get caregiver version, not the generic pack

**Mitigation:**
- If the test shows <0.5% response: stop before full rollout; diagnose whether the list, envelope, or offer is the issue by testing each variable separately
- Redirect budget to higher-performance digital channels while mail is iterated

**Learning signal:** Direct mail response rates by list segment, envelope variant, and opening give precise data on which message frame resonates with which audience — data that improves Facebook targeting as well.

## FM-7: Audience Backlash or Negative Coverage

**How it happens:** A journalist, advocacy group (Alzheimer's Association, caregiver advocacy organizations), or social media influencer portrays MemoryGuard as exploiting vulnerable people — specifically caregivers of dementia patients. Even factually compliant marketing can be characterized as predatory if the tone is perceived as fear-mongering or the category association (dementia) is seen as opportunistic.

**Severity:** High for brand reputation; potentially very high if it drives regulatory attention.

**Likelihood:** Low, but non-trivial. The supplement industry has a reputation for this behavior, and a new brand in this space will be scrutinized.

**Prevention:**
- Ensure caregiver-facing copy leads with empathy and dignity, not fear or exploitation
- Partner with or donate to a relevant caregiver or brain health organization (builds credibility and goodwill)
- Ensure clinical backing is genuine and documentable — don't overclaim the research
- Have a clear, publicly accessible FAQ about what MemoryGuard is and isn't

**Mitigation:**
- If a negative story runs: respond factually and promptly; do not go silent
- Prepare a statement in advance: the product, the evidence, the compliance, the ethical marketing commitments
- A proactive media outreach to health journalists before launch (not a press release — a genuine briefing) can preempt skeptical coverage

**Learning signal:** The specific criticism in any negative coverage tells you exactly what perception gap exists between your marketing intent and how the audience receives it. That gap should immediately inform copy revisions.

## FM-8: Payment Processor Rejects the Business

**How it happens:** Many mainstream payment processors (Stripe, PayPal standard accounts) restrict or refuse subscription supplement businesses due to high chargeback history in the category — even before you've processed a single transaction.

**Severity:** High. Cannot launch without a payment processor.

**Likelihood:** Moderate if using standard merchant accounts. Low if this is addressed before launch.

**Prevention:**
- Use a high-risk payment processor from the start (Authorize.net, NMI, or a dedicated supplement-friendly processor)
- Have a backup processor ready to activate — never rely on a single payment relationship
- Maintain a clean chargeback record from the first transaction

**Mitigation:**
- If rejected mid-launch: activate backup processor; migrate checkout
- If both are restricted: contact a payment consultant specializing in nutraceuticals

**Learning signal:** Payment processor relationships are a strategic business infrastructure decision, not a tactical one. Building redundancy early is cheaper than building it in a crisis.

## Priority Order for Pre-Launch Attention

| Priority | Failure mode | Why now |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | FM-3: Regulatory | Legal review must happen before anything publishes |
| 2 | FM-8: Payment processor | Must be solved before launch |
| 3 | FM-4: Chargebacks | Prevention is built into the confirmation/reminder email design |
| 4 | FM-1: Facebook suspension | Backup account and compliance review needed before ads run |
| 5 | FM-5: Low retention | Welcome sequence must be built before first subscriber arrives |
| 6 | FM-2: High CPA | Testing protocol addresses this; monitor closely at launch |
| 7 | FM-6: Direct mail non-response | Pre-launch split test before full rollout |
| 8 | FM-7: Backlash | Proactive media and partnership outreach pre-launch |
